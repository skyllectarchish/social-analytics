# Cross-Platform ROI

Cross-Platform ROI answers one question that every creator with both Instagram and YouTube wonders about: **does posting on Instagram actually drive people to subscribe on YouTube?**

---

## Why This Matters

Most creators treat Instagram and YouTube as separate worlds. You post Reels on one, long-form videos on the other, and you hope there's some audience overlap — but you have no way to *see* it.

This feature makes it visible. It overlays your YouTube subscriber growth with your Instagram Reel posting activity so you can spot patterns: days you posted a Reel, did subscriber counts on YouTube go up too?

---

## What You'll See

Go to **Cross-Platform** in the sidebar.

### The Main Chart

A large chart showing:
- **The red area** — your daily YouTube subscriber gains and losses (net subscribers) over the selected period
- **Purple vertical lines** — each line marks a day you posted an Instagram Reel

When you see purple lines clustering around spikes in the red area, that's your Instagram driving YouTube growth.

### The Correlation Badge

Above the chart, there's a badge like: **"Instagram → YouTube: 74% correlation"**

This number (called Pearson correlation) measures how strongly your Reel posting days line up with YouTube subscriber spikes. Here's how to read it:

| Correlation | What it means |
|---|---|
| 70%+ | Strong relationship — your Instagram audience is actively converting to YouTube subscribers |
| 30–70% | Moderate relationship — some overlap, but other factors are also driving growth |
| Under 30% | Weak relationship — your two audiences aren't crossing over much yet |

The badge is color-coded: green for strong, amber for moderate, gray for weak.

> **Important:** Correlation is not causation. A high score means your Reel days and YouTube subscriber days line up — but it doesn't prove your Reels *caused* the subs. Maybe you just post more actively across both platforms at the same time. Use this as a directional signal, not a definitive proof.

### Instagram Reels in Period

Below the chart, you'll see a list of the Reels you posted during the selected time window — thumbnail, date, and the first line of the caption. This helps you identify *which* specific Reels tend to correspond with subscriber spikes on YouTube.

---

## Changing the Date Range

Use the **30d / 90d / 180d** buttons in the top-right corner to change the time window. Longer windows give you a more reliable correlation score — the 90-day view is usually the most useful.

---

## How to Use This Effectively

**Look for patterns, not individual data points.** One spike doesn't mean much. Look for whether subscriber bumps *consistently* appear in the day or two after Reel posts.

**Study which Reels drive the most YouTube traffic.** Scroll through the Reels list and look at which ones have noticeable subscriber spikes after them. Was it a specific topic? A certain format? A Reel that directly mentioned your YouTube channel?

**If correlation is low:** Your Instagram and YouTube audiences may be different people. That's not necessarily a problem — but it does mean cross-promoting may not be as effective as you thought. You might need to be more explicit in your Reels about pointing people to YouTube ("full video on my channel," link in bio, etc.).

**If correlation is high:** You have a real cross-platform funnel. Double down on the Reel formats that are clearly converting. Consider adding a consistent CTA in every Reel pointing to YouTube.

---

## "No Data Yet" State

If you see an empty chart, check that:
- Both your **Instagram** and **YouTube** accounts are connected and have been synced
- You've been posting on both platforms during the selected date range

If Instagram isn't connected yet, the chart will show YouTube subscriber data only (without the Reel markers), and the correlation badge won't appear.

---

## What This Feature Cannot Tell You

- It can't tell you *which specific subscribers* came from Instagram — YouTube's API doesn't give us that granular attribution.
- It can't track traffic from Instagram links in your bio or story swipe-ups.
- It doesn't track TikTok, Twitter/X, or other platforms — just Instagram and YouTube.

The feature is a correlation report, not a full attribution system. But as a directional guide for where to focus your cross-platform energy, it's genuinely useful.
